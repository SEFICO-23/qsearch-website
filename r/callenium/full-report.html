Risk Categories

What the exposure looks like — without the specifics

Per QuantumSearch policy, we do not share specific vulnerability details, endpoints, or proof-of-exploitation in pre-engagement briefings. The following categories describe the shape of the exposure. Specifics and remediation paths are delivered under a signed engagement.

CriticalCATEGORY 1

Email identity integrity

The email authentication posture permits third parties to impersonate Callenium in outbound email with a high probability of inbox delivery. Practical effect: invoice fraud, policy-update phishing, pension-rollover fraud, Business Email Compromise. Industry median BEC loss: USD 200k–800k per incident.

CriticalCATEGORY 2

Public-facing administrative surface

Administrative interfaces typically restricted to a corporate VPN or IP allowlist are reachable from the public internet. These surfaces present brute-force, credential-stuffing, and known-CVE attack vectors. For a FINMA-adjacent firm, this posture is materially below sector baseline.

HighCATEGORY 3

Application authentication exposure

The primary website's authentication surface is publicly indexed and combined with enough public information to support credential-spraying. Per Verizon DBIR 2024, this pattern is the #1 initial access vector for financial-services breaches.

HighCATEGORY 4

Development / staging environment leakage

Non-production environments are publicly resolvable and discoverable via routine OSINT. Naming conventions visible in public records reveal at least one client relationship. Staging instances are typically less hardened than production — a preferred attacker pivot.

MediumCATEGORY 5

Third-party data-processor governance

Customer-facing application traffic traverses a third-party platform. Swiss FADP Art. 9 and EU GDPR Art. 28 require a documented processor relationship with verifiable security controls. Public evidence of governance is not observable.

MediumCATEGORY 6

Transport & response security hygiene

Public HTTP responses lack several security response headers that are now baseline for sites handling authenticated user traffic or sensitive forms. Inconsistency between authenticated and unauthenticated paths suggests ad-hoc rather than systematic hardening.

MediumCATEGORY 7

Content-management-system hardening

The production CMS exposes administrative-diagnostic information that should be restricted. Plugin composition includes premium components whose individual patch-cadence is not independently verifiable from public data — blind spot for incident preparedness.

Methodology

How this snapshot was produced

Everything in this document is derived from public records only. No active scan, no port probing, no brute force, no vulnerability-trigger testing. A Swiss StGB Art. 143bis (unauthorized access) violation is never acceptable — every QuantumSearch engagement begins only after signed authorization.

Certificate Transparency mining

Publicly-logged TLS certificate issuances queried via crt.sh. Reveals subdomains regardless of DNS visibility — the standard modern discovery technique.

Passive DNS

Authoritative and third-party DNS resolution for A, MX, TXT, NS, SOA, DMARC selectors, DKIM selectors. No zone transfer, no brute-force enumeration.

Single-GET HTTP reconnaissance

One GET request per publicly-indexed surface. Response headers and visible HTML/JSON parsed for technology fingerprinting and baseline hygiene review. Within normal crawler traffic.

OSINT cross-referencing

Wayback Machine, public GitHub APIs, Swiss commercial registry (Zefix). Validation of identified public footprint.

Regulatory mapping

Each technical finding mapped to Swiss FADP, FINMA Circular 2023/1, and EU GDPR clauses. Produces a regulatory-consequence ledger, not a CVE checklist.

Financial modelling

SimpleLoss lognormal Monte-Carlo simulation, 10,000 trials. Anchors from IBM Cost of Data Breach 2024 + Swiss jurisdictional fine ceilings + Verizon DBIR 2024 annualized rate of occurrence.
